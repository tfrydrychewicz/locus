# Knowledge management research for ADHD-affected engineers

**The core finding across decades of memory research is that ADHD creates an encoding bottleneck, not a storage or retrieval deficit.** Meta-analyses show working memory impairments of d = 0.69–2.15 in ADHD populations, meaning the gateway to long-term memory is severely constricted — but once information passes through, retention is normal. This single insight reframes the entire design challenge for an AI-native second brain: the system must compensate for weak encoding, not weak storage. Traditional knowledge management systems fail because they demand the very executive functions ADHD impairs — categorization, filing, consistent maintenance — while providing no help at the actual point of failure. The research synthesized below maps specific neurocognitive deficits to specific system design features, building an evidence-based blueprint for knowledge management as cognitive prosthesis.

---

## 1. The encoding bottleneck: how ADHD constrains knowledge formation

Working memory is the gateway through which all information must pass before entering long-term memory, and ADHD narrows this gateway substantially. Martinussen, Hayden, Hogg-Johnson, and Tannock (2005, *Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry*, 26 studies) found visuospatial working memory deficits at **d = 0.85–1.06** and verbal deficits at **d = 0.43–0.47** in children with ADHD. Kasper, Alderson, and Hudec (2012, *Clinical Psychology Review*) pushed these estimates higher through meta-regression, showing that construct-valid tasks with high central executive demands yield effect sizes of **d = 2.01–2.15**, with up to 85% of children with ADHD classified as working-memory-impaired. These deficits persist into adulthood: Alderson, Kasper, Hudec, and Patros (2013, *Neuropsychology*, 38 studies) confirmed moderate-magnitude effect sizes across adult samples, and Kofler et al. (2020, *Neuropsychology*) found a very large association between ADHD and working memory impairment (r = .63, d = 1.63) with 75% population non-overlap.

The critical downstream question is what happens to information that survives this bottleneck. **Skodzik, Holling, and Pedersen (2017, *Journal of Attention Disorders*)** provide the definitive answer through separate meta-analyses of memory acquisition and long-term memory in adult ADHD: adults with ADHD performed significantly worse on verbal long-term memory, but the deficit was **strongly statistically related to the memory acquisition deficit**, with no retrieval problems observable once encoding succeeded. Their conclusion — "memory deficits in adult ADHD reflect a learning deficit induced at the stage of encoding" — means the ADHD memory problem is fundamentally about getting information in, not getting it out. Knouse et al. (2023, *Frontiers in Psychology*) corroborated this: unmedicated college students with ADHD used significantly fewer deep encoding strategies, while medicated students performed comparably to controls.

Dopamine plays a dual role in this picture. Rossato, Bevilaqua, Izquierdo, Medina, and Cammarota (2009, *Science*) demonstrated that long-term memory persistence depends on VTA-hippocampus dopaminergic connections, with D1 receptor activation at a critical 12-hour post-learning window determining whether memories become persistent or decay. A D1 agonist at this window converted rapidly decaying memories into persistent ones. Linssen, Vuurman, Sambeth, and Riedel (2012, *Psychopharmacology*, N=19, double-blind crossover) found dose-related enhancement of declarative memory consolidation with methylphenidate. Dopamine dysregulation in ADHD thus impairs memory at two stages: the initial working memory encoding phase and the late-phase consolidation window.

Emotional salience provides a partial bypass. Krauel, Duzel, Hinrichs et al. (2007, *Biological Psychiatry*) found that emotional stimuli **significantly augmented memory performance** in ADHD adolescents, normalizing encoding to the level of healthy controls. The mechanism involves two distinct encoding pathways identified by Kensinger and Corkin (2004, *PNAS*): an automatic amygdala-hippocampal route for arousing information that survives divided attention, and a controlled prefrontal-hippocampal route for non-arousing information that is disrupted by it. The automatic pathway appears more intact in ADHD, explaining why emotionally salient content is preferentially encoded. Qasim et al. (2023, *Nature Human Behaviour*, N=148 with intracranial EEG) established the causal relationship: amygdala-hippocampal circuit activity directly supports prioritization of emotional memories.

**Design implication:** A knowledge management system for ADHD must reduce encoding demands at capture time, support multiple encoding modalities to increase depth of processing, and leverage emotional/contextual salience to improve retention. The system should never assume that saving equals remembering.

## 2. Prospective memory and the "remembering to remember" failure

The most practically devastating ADHD memory deficit may be prospective memory — remembering to act on stored intentions. Altgassen, Kretschmer, and Kliegel (2014, *Journal of Attention Disorders*, N=50) found a critical dissociation: **time-based prospective memory** (self-initiated monitoring, e.g., "review my notes at 3pm") showed large impairment in ADHD, while **event-based prospective memory** (externally triggered, e.g., "when I see this person, ask about X") showed no significant group differences. Fuermaier, Tucha, Koerts et al. (2013, *PLOS ONE*, N=90) confirmed large-scale impairment (Wilk's λ = 0.585, F(4,85) = 15.06, p < .001, **η² = .415**), with task planning showing the largest deficits. Altgassen, Scheres, and Edel (2019, *ADHD Attention Deficit and Hyperactivity Disorders*, N=53) found that real-world prospective memory deficits were more severe than laboratory measures suggested, and that **everyday prospective memory performance mediated the link between ADHD symptoms and procrastination**.

This pattern has direct architectural consequences. Time-based prospective memory requires intact self-monitoring — precisely what ADHD disrupts. Event-based prospective memory, triggered by environmental cues, is relatively preserved. A knowledge management system should therefore favor event-based surfacing (contextual triggers, location-based reminders, activity-based retrieval) over time-based review schedules. Spaced repetition systems, which are inherently time-based, face an implementation barrier that must be addressed through external cueing.

## 3. Why traditional knowledge management systems fail for ADHD

The "second brain" movement — Tiago Forte's PARA method, Zettelkasten, Building a Second Brain — represents sophisticated thinking about personal knowledge management, yet these systems create a fundamental paradox for ADHD users: **they require executive function to maintain the very system designed to compensate for executive function deficits**. Diamond (2013, *Annual Review of Psychology*) established that core executive functions — inhibition, working memory, cognitive flexibility — are precisely the functions required to categorize, file, tag, and maintain organizational systems. Sweller's (1988) cognitive load theory quantifies the cost: working memory holds approximately 4 items for concurrent processing for about 20 seconds, and every filing decision requires simultaneously holding the item in mind, evaluating possible categories, making a decision, and executing the action — a four-item working memory load before the content itself is even considered.

The cognitive cost of categorization follows Hick's Law: decision time increases logarithmically with the number of choices. Schneider and Anderson (2011, *Cognitive Psychology*) demonstrated that this law reflects associative interference during memory retrieval, meaning that choosing between many categories or tags is not merely slow but cognitively expensive. Malone (1983), in an early office organization study, identified "the cognitive difficulty of categorizing information" as a primary factor explaining organizational behavior and recommended systems do "as much automatic classification as possible." The paradox of choice compounds this: Iyengar and Lepper (2000, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*) showed in their field experiment that reducing options from 24 to 6 increased purchase rates **tenfold** (3% to 30%), with Chernev, Böckenholt, and Goodman (2015, *Journal of Consumer Psychology*, 99 studies) confirming that choice overload intensifies when preferences are uncertain and when optimal choice matters greatly — both characteristic of knowledge organization decisions.

Information hoarding emerges as a predictable ADHD coping pattern. Morein-Zamir et al. (2022, *Journal of Psychiatric Research*, N=178) found that **approximately 20% of ADHD patients showed clinically significant hoarding symptoms versus 2% of controls** — a tenfold difference — with inattention specifically predicting hoarding severity. Van Bennekom et al. (2015, *BMJ Case Reports*) documented the first case of digital hoarding, establishing it as a subtype of hoarding disorder associated with ADHD traits. The "collector's fallacy" describes the ADHD-relevant pattern precisely: the dopamine reward of saving and bookmarking creates the illusion of learning without actual processing. Bergman and Whittaker (2016, *The Science of Managing Our Digital Stuff*, MIT Press) found that people who actively organize their files have **65% lower retrieval failure rates**, but this demands the organizational executive function ADHD impairs — a catch-22.

Tool abandonment completes the failure cycle. While no peer-reviewed studies quantify PKM tool abandonment rates specifically (a significant research gap), practitioner evidence is consistent and voluminous. Leroy (2009, *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*) established that task switching creates "attention residue" degrading subsequent performance; knowledge workers switch applications approximately 1,100 times daily. The ADHD abandonment pattern — download, feel hopeful, use for a week, miss a day, feel guilty, abandon — reflects a neurological reality, not a character flaw.

## 4. Evidence-based approaches to knowledge capture

If encoding is the bottleneck, capture design is the highest-leverage intervention. The evidence supports three principles: reduce friction, leverage verbal strengths, and embed rich context.

**Zero-friction capture** addresses the fundamental tension between capture completeness and executive function cost. Sweller's cognitive load framework predicts that reducing extraneous load at the moment of capture preserves working memory for germane processing of the information itself. Research on written expression in ADHD provides a concrete rationale for voice-based capture: Friedman et al. (2018, *Journal of Abnormal Child Psychology*, N=60) found that phonological short-term memory and oral expression collectively mediate the relationship between ADHD and written expression deficits, while 45% of children with ADHD experience significant written expression difficulties despite relatively preserved verbal expression. Berninger et al. (2006) established through the "Simple View of Writing" model that all writing processes are constrained by working memory — speech-to-text bypasses the transcription bottleneck entirely, freeing working memory for content generation.

**Context-rich capture** builds on Tulving and Thomson's (1973, *Psychological Review*) encoding specificity principle: memory retrieval improves when encoding context matches retrieval context. Godden and Baddeley's (1975) classic study demonstrated this dramatically — words learned underwater were recalled better underwater. Choi et al. (2025, *Frontiers in Psychology*) extended this to real-world settings using smartphone GPS tracking over 5 weeks, finding **stronger context-dependent memory effects at low-frequency locations** with benefits increasing with context dwell time. Bergman (2003, *JASIST*) connected this directly to personal information management through his "subjective context principle": information should be retrieved in the same context in which it was previously used, explicitly citing Tulving and Thomson as the theoretical foundation. Systems that strip context during capture — filing into abstract categories disconnected from the original situation — may actively impair later retrieval for ADHD users who rely more heavily on contextual and episodic memory cues.

**Active recall and the testing effect** provide strong evidence for retrieval practice in ADHD populations. Roediger and Karpicke (2006, *Psychological Science*) established that a single retrieval attempt can more than double long-term retention (80% vs. 34% after one week). Knouse et al. (2016) demonstrated that **both ADHD and control groups benefited equally from retrieval practice**, and Knouse et al. (2020, *Learning and Instruction*, N=179) replicated this finding with key-term definitions over one week. However, Minear et al. (2023, *Frontiers in Psychology*, N=72) added a critical nuance: retrieval practice benefits ADHD students but **does not fully compensate for encoding deficits in unmedicated students**. The practical implication is that retrieval practice is necessary but not sufficient — the encoding quality must also be addressed.

Spaced repetition presents a theoretical promise and a practical challenge. While the spacing effect is robustly supported in the general population (Cepeda et al., 2006), no controlled trial has specifically tested spaced repetition effectiveness in ADHD populations. The implementation barrier is significant: spaced repetition systems like Anki are inherently time-based, requiring consistent daily engagement — precisely the prospective memory function most impaired in ADHD. Adi-Japha et al. (2017, *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*, N=49) suggest that shorter training sessions may be more beneficial for ADHD, and current recommendations favor **10–15 minute focused sessions** on small item sets rather than longer continuous practice.

## 5. Information retrieval and the ADHD cognitive style

Lansdale (1988, *Applied Ergonomics*) established that personal information management challenges are fundamentally psychological, devolving upon processes of recall, recognition, and categorization. His key insight — that diligent filing does not always enable successful retrieval because hierarchical classification systems have overlapping and ambiguous categories — describes the universal "I know I saved it somewhere" problem. Teevan, Adar, Jones, and Potts (2007, *SIGIR*, N=233) quantified re-finding behavior: **as many as 40% of all web queries are re-finding queries**, with original search terms misremembered 30% of the time.

ADHD cognition naturally favors associative over hierarchical retrieval. White and Shah (2006, 2011, 2016, *Personality and Individual Differences*; *Creativity Research Journal*) demonstrated across multiple studies that ADHD individuals show **wider semantic activation**, produce associates of lower semantic relatedness, and score higher in originality and flexibility on creative tasks. Their 2016 study (N=60) confirmed through latent semantic analysis that ADHD participants' higher creative flexibility was mediated by greater semantic distance in word associations. Hoogman et al. (2020, *Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews*, 31 studies) concluded that "people with ADHD seem to have a more flexible association network." This divergent cognitive style means hierarchical folder structures work against the ADHD brain's natural retrieval patterns, while associative, graph-based, and semantic retrieval methods align with them.

Semantic search directly addresses the ADHD retrieval problem. When ADHD individuals recall the gist of saved information but not the specific terms used at capture time, keyword search fails. Natural language interfaces reduce this barrier: Yeralan (2023) found users required **5.2 hours** to become proficient with traditional interfaces versus **1.7 hours** with natural language interfaces — a 67% reduction. Conversational search systems maintain context over multi-turn interactions, and as noted in a comprehensive ACM survey (Mo et al., 2024), they "potentially reduce cognitive load by assisting the user in building a query over multiple conversational steps." The ReFinder system (Kajalekar, 2017) demonstrated the value of context-based retrieval in an 8-week study: users completed re-finding tasks in **16.5 seconds** with contextual cues versus **86.3 seconds** without.

The tip-of-the-tongue phenomenon, first systematically studied by Brown and McNeill (1966, *Journal of Verbal Learning and Verbal Behavior*), reveals that partial information — first letters, syllable counts, semantic neighbors — is often available even when full retrieval fails. Brown (1991, *Psychological Bulletin*) found TOT states occur about once a week, frequently involve proper names, enable access to the target's first letter roughly half the time, and resolve during the experience approximately 50% of the time. Providing **multiple retrieval pathways** — semantic, temporal, spatial, social — maximizes resolution probability and directly serves ADHD users whose encoding patterns may be atypical.

## 6. Note-taking, synthesis, and knowledge connection

Shimko and James (2025, *Educational Psychology*, N=152) found that for ADHD students, **typing and handwriting were equally effective** — both far superior to taking no notes — but students with ADHD exhibited slower handwriting speeds with no differences in typing speed. A companion classroom study (Shimko, 2025, *Applied Cognitive Psychology*, N=229) found that for students with higher ADHD-Inattentive symptoms, **laptop and tablet notes led to higher quiz scores** than handwritten notes, likely because reduced motor demands freed cognitive resources for encoding. This challenges Mueller and Oppenheimer's (2014, *Psychological Science*) "pen is mightier than the keyboard" finding, which Morehead, Dunlosky, and Rawson (2019, *Educational Psychology Review*) already showed failed to replicate with small, nonsignificant effects in direct replication attempts.

The generation effect — self-generated information is remembered better than passively read information, first demonstrated by Slamecka and Graf (1978, *Journal of Experimental Psychology*, 5 experiments, N=96) with a meta-analytic effect size of **d = 0.40** across 86 studies (Bertsch et al., 2007) — has clear implications for ADHD knowledge retention. Forcing deeper processing through generation should benefit ADHD learners, but Krauel et al. (2009, *Neuropsychologia*, N=33) found that ADHD patients show deficits in translating pre-stimulus mobilization to successful memory formation for neutral stimuli. The generation effect works for ADHD only when combined with sufficient engagement or emotional salience.

**Mind mapping** has the strongest controlled evidence for visual knowledge organization in ADHD. Piotrowska et al. (2021, *International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health*, N=45 children with ADHD) found that mind map training was the only intervention that significantly improved response inhibition (F(2,42) = 10.02, p = .001), with **32% of progress attributable to mind map training (ω² = 0.32)** — a large effect size. An ACM UIST 2024 paper documented that mind mapping software effectively stimulates motivation, aids information organization, and enhances learning in ADHD students. No peer-reviewed RCTs on graph-based digital tools like Obsidian or Roam Research for ADHD populations exist, but the theoretical alignment with ADHD's wider semantic activation (White & Shah, 2006–2016) is strong: bidirectional linking organizes knowledge by association rather than hierarchy, matching the ADHD brain's natural retrieval patterns.

## 7. AI-assisted knowledge management as cognitive prosthesis

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) architectures, introduced by Lewis et al. (2020, *NeurIPS*), combine parametric and non-parametric memory for language generation, with models of only a few hundred million parameters outperforming 11-billion-parameter closed-book language models on open-domain QA. The architecture is directly analogous to a cognitive prosthetic: the parametric model provides reasoning while the non-parametric store provides memory, mirroring how an ADHD brain might retain reasoning capacity while offloading storage. Mo et al. (2024, *CIKM*) demonstrated that personal textual knowledge bases integrated with LLMs generate more appropriate personalized queries, the closest existing research to "ask your notes" systems.

Jang et al. (2024, *PACM HCI/CSCW*) conducted qualitative analysis of 61 neurodivergent Reddit communities and identified **20 specific LLM use cases across 5 core areas**, including cognitive offloading, thought organization, and reducing executive function demands. However, a critical tension exists. An EEG study (brainonllm.com, 2025, N=54, 4 sessions over 4 months) found that brain connectivity systematically scaled down with external AI support: brain-only conditions showed the strongest neural networks, search engines intermediate engagement, and LLM assistance the weakest coupling. A meta-analysis of 47 experiments (N > 3,200) found that AI summarization used as a substitute reduced conceptual application scores from **72% to 39%** — a 46% decline — while AI used as a scaffold after engagement preserved performance at 68%. The design implication is precise: AI should augment retrieval and synthesis after initial encoding engagement, not replace it.

Automatic tagging and classification directly address the executive function demands of organization. McKinsey data estimates knowledge workers spend **1.8 hours daily** (9 hours weekly) searching for and gathering information. Auto-classification using semantic classifiers improves both the speed and quality of metadata while eliminating categorization decisions entirely. For ADHD users, this addresses the Hick's Law problem — removing the logarithmic decision cost of manual filing.

Passive and ambient capture completes the prosthetic architecture. Berry et al. (2007, *Neuropsychological Rehabilitation*) demonstrated that SenseCam — a wearable automatic camera — enabled a memory-impaired patient to recall **approximately 80% of recent events** versus 49% with written diary, with retention maintained at 11 months. Sellen and Whittaker (2010, *Communications of the ACM*) cautioned that total capture approaches must "work in synergy with human memory, rather than as its replacement," echoing the AI summarization findings about cognitive disengagement.

## 8. Theoretical frameworks: externalization as architecture, not accommodation

Clark and Chalmers' (1998, *Analysis*) extended mind thesis argues that external objects functioning as cognitive processes constitute part of the cognitive system itself. Their "Otto's notebook" thought experiment — where an Alzheimer's patient's notebook serves the same functional role as biological memory — is directly analogous to ADHD: where biological working memory and prospective memory are impaired, external tools serve the same architectural role as intact internal systems, not as compensatory additions.

Barkley (1997, *Psychological Bulletin*; 2012, *Executive Functions*, Guilford Press) formalized this into operational principles: externalize information at key points of performance, make covert information overt, externalize time, and externalize motivation. His **30% rule** — children with ADHD demonstrate approximately 30% developmental delay in executive functions — quantifies the mismatch between internal capacity and environmental demands. Applied to knowledge management: a 30-year-old engineer with ADHD may have the executive function capacity for information organization of a 21-year-old, making systems designed for peak adult executive function inherently inaccessible.

Wegner's (1987) transactive memory systems describe how social groups collectively encode, store, and retrieve knowledge by distributing memory across members. Wegner, Erber, and Raymond (1991, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*) demonstrated that established couples remembered more items (M = 31.40) than ad hoc pairs (M = 27.64, p < .05). ADHD individuals may naturally rely on transactive memory — using partners and colleagues as external memory stores — making the "ask your notes" paradigm a technological extension of an existing coping strategy. No peer-reviewed research directly applies transactive memory theory to ADHD populations, representing a significant gap.

## 9. Information overload in the engineering management context

Engineering managers face a particularly acute version of the knowledge management challenge. McKinsey estimates knowledge workers spend **28% of their workweek** managing email and **20%** searching for internal information. Gloria Mark (UC Irvine, published in *Attention Span*, 2023) found average time on any screen has declined to **47 seconds**, with **23 minutes and 15 seconds** required to regain deep focus after interruption — and workers self-interrupt 49% of the time. Gartner's 2023 survey of approximately 1,000 employees found **47% have trouble finding information relevant to their roles** and **32% report making incorrect decisions due to lack of available insight**. IDC estimated Fortune 500 companies lose **$31.5 billion annually** from forgotten organizational knowledge.

For an ADHD engineering manager, these challenges compound with the neurocognitive deficits described above. Meeting capture is particularly challenging: working memory deficits impair real-time information retention, and the Ebbinghaus forgetting curve operates on already-weakly-encoded material. Knowledge loss from turnover is a related concern — a systematic review of 91 empirical studies (Emerald Insight, 2023) documented that tacit knowledge loss from departing employees causes faults in product design, time losses, and innovation failures, with costs estimated at **up to 213% of the departing individual's salary**. Personal knowledge bases that are searchable, shareable, and AI-indexed serve as organizational assets that reduce bus-factor risk while simultaneously functioning as individual cognitive prosthetics.

## 10. Design principles: building the ADHD-native knowledge system

The research converges on five core design principles, each grounded in specific evidence.

**Progressive disclosure** reduces cognitive overload by presenting only task-relevant information at each layer. Hwang et al. (2020) found the optimal number of high-legibility initial options is **3**, with progressive revelation to deeper layers. An ACM study (2025) with 22 ADHD participants confirmed progressive disclosure as a key feature of preferred ADHD-friendly interfaces. This directly implements Sweller's cognitive load theory: minimize extraneous load by showing only what is needed at each moment.

**Smart defaults and automation** leverage the most powerful behavioral insight in the research. Johnson and Goldstein (2003, *Science*) showed defaults can shift outcomes by **30–50 percentage points** — from 42% opt-in to 82% opt-out even when the effort to change was identical. Applied to knowledge management: auto-tagging, auto-linking, auto-categorization should be the default, with manual override available but never required. Every decision eliminated is working memory preserved for actual knowledge work.

**Forgiving systems** must accommodate the interest-based nervous system of ADHD, which produces variable engagement patterns. Systems should auto-save state and context so returning after a gap is seamless, avoid punishing inconsistency (no streaks that reset, no data that becomes inaccessible), and provide re-entry affordances — summaries of where the user left off and what has changed. The arXiv framework for neurodivergent-aware productivity (2025, N=25) found AI tools should accommodate neurodivergent rhythms rather than emulate neurotypical focus patterns.

**Associative retrieval over hierarchical organization** aligns with ADHD's wider semantic activation patterns (White & Shah, 2006–2016). Semantic search, graph-based knowledge structures, and conversational interfaces all support the ADHD brain's natural retrieval style. The system should support multiple retrieval pathways — temporal, semantic, contextual, social — following the tip-of-the-tongue research showing that partial cues across modalities maximize retrieval success.

**Encoding support at capture** addresses the fundamental bottleneck. Voice capture leverages preserved verbal expression while bypassing written expression deficits. Contextual metadata — location, time, project, emotional state, co-occurring activity — should be captured automatically to serve as future retrieval cues per the encoding specificity principle. Active recall prompts after capture leverage the testing effect (equally effective for ADHD per Knouse et al., 2016, 2020) to strengthen encoding. Jamieson et al. (2013, *Neuropsychological Rehabilitation*, meta-analysis of 7 group studies, N=147) found cognitive prosthetic technology produces a **large effect size of d = 1.27** for improving everyday memory task performance — confirming the viability of this approach.

## Synthesis: mapping evidence to an AI-native second brain

The research makes a clear case that AI-native knowledge management is not merely a convenience for ADHD users but an architectural necessity — a cognitive prosthetic that compensates for specific, quantified deficits. The encoding bottleneck (d = 0.69–2.15 for working memory) demands zero-friction, context-rich capture. The prospective memory deficit (η² = .415) demands event-based rather than time-based surfacing. The organizational paradox demands automated classification rather than manual filing. The associative cognitive style demands semantic, graph-based retrieval rather than hierarchical navigation. The emotional encoding advantage demands systems that capture and leverage affective context.

Integration with a companion productivity system creates a unified externalization architecture consistent with Barkley's framework: knowledge management handles the "what" (information encoding and retrieval), while productivity management handles the "when" and "how" (time externalization and motivation externalization). Clark and Chalmers' extended mind thesis provides the philosophical justification; Barkley's externalization principle provides the clinical framework; and the quantitative evidence reviewed here provides the engineering specification.

The largest remaining research gaps are the absence of controlled trials specifically evaluating RAG-based knowledge systems, spaced repetition, and auto-classification for ADHD populations. The closest available evidence — cognitive prosthetic technology producing d = 1.27 in memory task performance (Jamieson et al., 2013), and the 10x difference in retrieval speed with contextual cues (16.5s vs. 86.3s, Kajalekar 2017) — suggests the magnitude of potential benefit is large. Building to this evidence while these specific gaps close is both justified and urgent.
